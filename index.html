About me

Hi there!
My name is Neha Rajagopalan and I'm a music and Electroencephalography (EEG) researcher. I'm a graduate student in the M.S in Music Technology program at Georgia Institute of Technology. Currently, I am also a Predoctoral Research Associate at the EdNeuro Initiative at Stanford University and am advised by Dr. Blair Kaneshiro and by Dr. Bruce McCandliss. I did my undergraduate degree in B.E in Electronics and Communications Engineering at BITS Pilani Hyderabad Campus, India. I am also a Carnatic music vocalist and a Bharatanatyam dancer.

My research interests include Music Perception and Cognition, Cognitive Neuroscience, and Machine Learning. I'm extremely fascinated by the effects of music on the human brain, particularly from the perspective of emotions and engagement, and seek to apply this knowledge in domains such as education, health and well-being. I also enjoy integrating neural and physiological signals such as EEG, and EMG into music performances by sonifying them.

Music Induced Emotion Classification on EEG Data using Machine Learning

This project classified EEG signals acquired from a music-listening task (DEAP Dataset) into 4 emotions (Happy, Angry, Sad, Calm) where each emotion represented one quadrant from Russell's Circumplex model. K-Means Clustering was used to segregate the data points into the 4 emotions based on the valence and arousal ratings given by subjects. The preprocessing pipeline included lowpass filtering, notch filtering for line noise removal, bad channel removal, channel interpolation and ICA analysis. The features for the classification model were emotion features such as asymmetrical power spectra of 5 frequency bands (alpha, beta, theta, gamma, and delta) that were extracted using 4 configurations of electrodes (DASM, RASM, PSD30 and PSD24). Support Vector Machines (SVM), K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) and Convolutional Neural Network(CNN) models were used for classification

TERRY - The Theremin Playing Robot

TERRY is an interactive theremin playing robot who is controlled by the facial expressions of a music performer. When the performer smiles, TERRY plays happy and distinct staccato notes of a song and when the performer frowns, TERRY switches to slow and continuous legato notes of the song. Facial muscle activity is recorded via EMG sensors attached below the eye and above the eyebrow of the performer to determine smiling and frowning.
